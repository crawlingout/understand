 How to learn languages with audiobooks?

Get both audio and text versions of some really interesting book and follow this procedure:

  • Start listening to the audio without reading the text. It's really important not to read the text at this moment - the purpose of this exercise is to stimulate the part of the brain responsible for recognizing words in sounds (listening while reading would be like training for a cycling race on a motorbike). Try to catch as much as you can. When you get lost, pause the audio.

  • Now read the part you just listened to and figure out the incomprehensible parts. You can listen to it again in order to hear the words in previously unintelligible sounds. Click on words you don't know to see their translations.

  • Repeat. The goal is to expose your brain to uninterrupted sequences of speech that you can barely catch. Put yourself under the pressure to stimulate your ability to understand instantly without translating in your head.

TIP: Every time you encounter a word that needs to be translated, you will be tempted to try to carve it into your memory, just like you do when you are memorizing words from a vocabulary list. Don't do it! Your only goal is to understand the story. The more you immerse yourself in what is said instead of focusing on how it is said, the more words will get stuck in your head.

 What is the A/L ratio and why is it tracked?

The A/L (Audio/Listening) ratio is the ratio between the length of an audio track and the actual time you spend listening to that track. This ratio is a great indicator of your ability to understand. If you pause every few seconds and listen to each sentence multiple times, you will need 10 minutes to get through 1 minute of audio - your A/L ratio will be 10%. If you understand everything without difficulty, you can listen continuously at 100% ratio. In short, hight A/L ratio is good, low A/L ratio is bad. If your daily A/L ratio is increasing over time, it means that you are improving.
